Living abroad is exhilarating. Building wealth while living abroad is complicated.

Here's the no-BS guide for expat investors.

## The Unique Challenges of Expat Investing

### 1. US Citizens Face FATCA Everywhere
If you're American, your worldwide income is taxable in the US — regardless of where you live. Many foreign brokerages refuse to open accounts for Americans because of FATCA compliance costs.

**Solution**: Keep a US brokerage account. Charles Schwab International is the gold standard for expats — they actively support overseas residents.

### 2. PFIC Rules Kill International Fund Returns for Americans
If you buy non-US mutual funds or ETFs as an American expat, you may face punitive PFIC (Passive Foreign Investment Company) tax treatment.

**Solution**: Use US-domiciled ETFs (Vanguard, iShares, SPDR) even while living abroad. VTI, VXUS, and BND are all US-domiciled and PFIC-safe.

### 3. Currency Risk on Both Sides
Your income may be in foreign currency, your US bills in USD, and your investments in yet another currency.

**Solution**: Match assets to liabilities. If you'll retire in Europe, hold some EUR-denominated assets.

## Best Brokerages for Expats

1. **Charles Schwab International** — Best overall. Works in 175+ countries, no foreign transaction fees.
2. **Interactive Brokers** — Best for non-Americans needing multi-exchange access.
3. **TD Ameritrade** — Works for Americans in many countries (check your specific country).

**Avoid**: Robinhood, Webull, and most neo-brokers — they typically don't work for non-US residents.

## The Tax Conversation You Must Have

Before any investment decisions as an expat, consult:
1. **A US-qualified CPA with expat experience** (not a generalist)
2. **A local tax advisor** in your country of residence

The cost of good advice ($500–2,000/year) is tiny compared to the mistakes it prevents.

## Bottom Line

Being an expat doesn't have to mean sacrificing investment returns. Use US-domiciled ETFs, maintain a US brokerage account, and get proper tax advice. The complexity is manageable — the opportunity is real.
